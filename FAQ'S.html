Astronomy FAQ'S

What is Astronomy?
Astronomy is the scientific study of celestial objects such as stars, planets, comets, and galaxies, and phenomena that originate outside Earth's atmosphere.
What is a black hole?
A black hole is a region in space where gravity is so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape from it.
How was the universe formed?
The universe is believed to have formed about 13.8 billion years ago in a massive expansion called the Big Bang.
What are exoplanets?
Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system.
Is there life beyond Earth?
While there is no confirmed evidence yet, scientists are actively researching and exploring the possibility of life beyond Earth.
What is a supernova?
A supernova is the explosive death of a massive star, resulting in a powerful burst of radiation and the creation of heavy elements that are spread into space.
What is dark matter?
Dark matter is an invisible substance that doesn't emit light or energy, yet it makes up about 27% of the universe and affects the movement of galaxies through its gravity.
How do telescopes help astronomers?
Telescopes allow astronomers to observe distant celestial objects by collecting light, radio waves, or other signals, helping us learn about stars, galaxies, and the structure of the universe.
What is the Milky Way?
The Milky Way is the galaxy that contains our solar system. It is a barred spiral galaxy composed of billions of stars, planets, and cosmic dust, and it appears as a milky band in the night sky.
What is the Hubble Space Telescope?
The Hubble Space Telescope is a powerful observatory launched into Earth's orbit in 1990. It has provided some of the most detailed images of distant galaxies, nebulae, and other cosmic phenomena, helping scientists better understand the universe.
